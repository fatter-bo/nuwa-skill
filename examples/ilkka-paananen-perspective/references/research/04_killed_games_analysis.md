# Research File 04: Killed Games & Project Decision Analysis

## Source Type: Mixed (journalism, official statements)

### Scale of Project Kills
- As of 2023, Supercell has killed 30+ games (Ilkka Paananen's count)
- Ratio: roughly 10 games killed for every 1 released
- In one two-year period: released 1 game (Clash Royale), killed 9 projects
- Many killed before public announcement or alpha stage

### Known Killed Games (partial list from reporting):
- Gunshine (browser game, pre-mobile pivot, 2011)
- Smash Land (turn-based PVP, polished, positive early feedback)
- Rush Wars (2019, killed after beta)
- Multiple unnamed internal prototypes

### How Kill Decisions Are Made:
1. **Team decides, not management** - The development team makes the final call
2. **Sauna deliberation** (Smash Land case) - Team asked: "If we could develop one game for the next years, is this it?"
3. **Metric-based but not purely data-driven** - Retention targets exist, but gut feeling matters
4. **Long-term retention is the key metric** - "Not just early retention, but always focus on the long term"
5. **"Would people play this for years?"** - The fundamental question

### Kill Criteria (synthesized):
- Game doesn't meet retention targets in soft-launch (Canada/select markets)
- Team loses belief that the game can become a "years-long" experience
- Polished and fun isn't enough - must show signals of decade-scale engagement
- Better to kill a good game than ship a non-great one

### The Champagne Ritual:
- Friday all-hands meeting
- Game lead presents learnings to entire company
- Team toasts with champagne
- Focus on what was learned, not the failure itself
- Creates psychological safety for future risk-taking
- "We don't celebrate the failure, rather the learnings that come from those failures"

### Boom Beach Decision (counter-example):
- 9 of 10 team leads wanted to cancel it
- Paananen chose to preserve culture over business logic
- Quote: "even if it would be the right decision from a business perspective, the price that we pay is the culture"
- Preserved team's autonomy to continue

### Clash Royale Skepticism (counter-example):
- Paananen: "I wasn't a big believer in the original prototype"
- Autonomous team structure allowed it to succeed
- Grossed over $2 billion in two years

### Rush Wars Kill:
- Soft-launched in beta
- Didn't meet Supercell's quality bar for global launch
- Team decided to kill it despite public beta

### Key Insight: The kill culture is NOT about punishment - it's about maintaining an absurdly high quality bar and allowing teams the dignity of making their own decisions about their work.
