## 1. Career-Defining Decisions

### Decision 1: Leave Consulting for Startups (1999)
- **Context**: Stable career at BCG (6 years), dotcom boom emerging
- **Choice**: Joined Swedish startup Spray as Managing Director
- **Outcome**: Grew from 20 to 800 employees, sold to Lycos in 2000
- **Pattern**: Willingness to trade certainty for upside; "choose the path of the unknown"

### Decision 2: Go All-In on Midasplayer/King (August 2003)
- **Context**: Made ~$300K from uDate sale, comfortable but not wealthy
- **Choice**: Invested all savings, sold car, gave up apartment, moved into friend's guest room
- **Outcome**: 2.5 years of couch-surfing before company stabilized
- **Pattern**: Entrepreneur ≠ investor — "you have to go all in"

### Decision 3: Buy King.com Domain (2005)
- **Context**: Company had 250K DAU, limited capital
- **Choice**: Spent $200K-$300K on domain from English teacher named King
- **Outcome**: Rebranded from Midasplayer to King.com — cleaner brand for global scaling
- **Pattern**: Invest in brand infrastructure even when capital-constrained

### Decision 4: The Facebook Pivot (2009-2011)
- **Context**: Yahoo partnership collapsing (45% user loss in 12 months), Facebook/Zynga rising
- **Choice**: Split 110-person company into two halves — revenue maintenance + five experimental teams
- **Outcome**: 2.5-year reinvention; Bubble Saga (March 2011) reached 1M DAU in 3 months
- **Pattern**: Parallel experimentation under existential pressure; honest communication throughout

### Decision 5: Mobile-First for Candy Crush (2012)
- **Context**: Candy Crush already successful on Facebook web
- **Choice**: Aggressive mobile launch with cross-platform save (Facebook ↔ mobile)
- **Outcome**: First mover in cross-platform casual gaming; explosive growth
- **Pattern**: Platform transitions are windows of opportunity — move fast when the window opens

### Decision 6: $100M+ Marketing Bet (Q1 2013)
- **Context**: Candy Crush showing strong unit economics
- **Choice**: Massive marketing investment justified by chessboard strategy data
- **Outcome**: Revenue went from $165M (2012) → $1.85B (2013) — 11X in 12 months
- **Pattern**: Scale aggressively only when unit economics are proven at every step

### Decision 7: IPO on NYSE (March 2014)
- **Context**: $7.08B valuation, largest mobile gaming IPO in US
- **Choice**: Go public despite "one-hit wonder" skepticism
- **Outcome**: Stock dropped 15% day one; never recovered to IPO price
- **Pattern**: IPO was defensive (liquidity for early investors) not offensive (growth capital)

### Decision 8: Accept Activision Acquisition ($5.9B, 2016)
- **Context**: Public market undervaluing King; stock at ~$15 vs $22.50 IPO
- **Choice**: Accept Activision's $18/share offer
- **Outcome**: King integrated as Activision's mobile division; Candy Crush franchise sustained
- **Pattern**: Know when the public market narrative is working against you; strategic buyer values differently

### Decision 9: Step Down as CEO (May 2019)
- **Context**: 16 years as CEO, company now inside Activision Blizzard
- **Choice**: Transition to Chairman, then full exit in 2020
- **Outcome**: Founded Sweet Capital, shifted to investor/advisor role
- **Pattern**: Knowing when your chapter is complete

## 2. Decision Framework Analysis

### The Zacconi Decision Stack

1. **Data before intuition**: Every major scaling decision backed by cohort metrics
2. **Parallel bets under constraint**: When facing existential threat, don't pick one path — run five experiments simultaneously
3. **All-in commitment**: Once data validates a direction, commit fully (sell the car, spend $100M on marketing)
4. **Patient escalation**: Chessboard strategy — double down only when each step proves out
5. **Honest assessment**: "Don't tell beautiful lies" — to yourself, investors, or team
6. **Team > individual**: Hire better than yourself, then get out of the way

### Recurring Patterns
- **Platform arbitrage**: Web → Facebook → Mobile — always be first to the next platform
- **Genre recombination**: Don't invent genres, combine proven elements distinctively
- **Retention over monetization**: Long-term player value always beats short-term conversion
- **Profitability as compass**: "I believe in profitable companies" — unit economics first, scale second
